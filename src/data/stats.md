
A Pearson product–moment correlation analysis was conducted to examine the relationship between dimensions of English language anxiety (communication anxiety, fear of negative evaluation, test anxiety, and English class anxiety) and selected predictors of language anxiety (self-confidence, language proficiency, peer influence, and classroom environment). Descriptive statistics indicated moderate levels across all constructs, suggesting that respondents generally experienced noticeable but not extreme levels of anxiety and its predictors, as reflected in the survey indicators (e.g., nervousness during speaking tasks, fear of peer judgment, and difficulties with language proficiency).

Correlation results revealed that most relationships between English language anxiety dimensions and their predictors were not statistically significant (p > .05). For instance, communication anxiety was not significantly related to self-confidence (r = −.221, p = .721), language proficiency (r = .308, p = .614), peer influence (r = −.280, p = .648), or classroom environment (r = −.511, p = .379). Similarly, fear of negative evaluation, test anxiety, and English class anxiety showed no significant associations with the predictor variables, despite some moderate to strong coefficients (e.g., communication anxiety and test anxiety, r = .866, p = .058), indicating that these relationships did not reach statistical significance.

However, a statistically significant and strong negative relationship was found between self-confidence and language proficiency, r(3) = −.896, p = .039. This suggests that as perceived language proficiency increases, self-reported lack of confidence decreases, or conversely, lower proficiency is associated with diminished self-confidence. This finding aligns with the indicators, where difficulties in vocabulary, grammar, and sentence construction are closely tied to reduced confidence in participating in English-related tasks.

Overall, the findings indicate that the null hypothesis is largely accepted, as there is no sufficient evidence to support a significant relationship between English language anxiety and its predictors, except for the relationship between self-confidence and language proficiency. Nonetheless, the presence of relatively strong but nonsignificant correlations suggests potential underlying relationships that may become significant with a larger sample size. Therefore, while the current results do not provide robust statistical support for the hypothesized relationships, they highlight the complex interplay between affective and linguistic factors in shaping learners’ experiences of English language anxiety.
